How to Read More Books: 15 Strategies That Actually Work

A practical guide to reading more books — from building a reading habit to reading faster without losing comprehension.

Most people who say they want to read more books have already tried the obvious things: buying more books, setting ambitious goals, downloading apps. And yet the stack on the nightstand grows without getting shorter. The problem is rarely motivation. It's friction — small but cumulative obstacles that make picking up a book slightly harder than picking up a phone.

Reading more is worth the effort. The evidence is strong that sustained, deep reading builds vocabulary, empathy, and the capacity for complex thought in ways that shorter-form content doesn't. More practically: the ideas in books are often the difference between understanding your situation and not understanding it. A single book read at the right moment can change the trajectory of a career, a relationship, or a life.

These fifteen strategies address the real barriers — environmental, psychological, and practical — that stand between most people and the reading life they want.

Build the Foundation

1. Set a reading goal — then reverse-engineer the habit

Annual reading goals ("I want to read 24 books this year") are motivating but they live at the wrong level. They tell you what you want to achieve, not what daily behaviour will get you there.

The reverse-engineering exercise is simple: 24 books at an average of 250 pages is 6,000 pages. At 25 pages a day — about 20 minutes for most readers — you'll hit that goal in 240 days, with 125 days of slack for life. Twenty-five pages a day is not a challenge. It's a decision.

The power of the goal is that it lets you track. Use a simple spreadsheet or a notebook with monthly page counts. When you're behind, you know it. When you're ahead, you feel it.

2. Build a reading environment

Physical environment shapes behaviour more than willpower does. The environments that produce reading are ones where books are visible, accessible, and associated with a specific time or location.

The practical steps: keep a book on your desk, another by your bed, and another in whatever bag you carry. Remove phones from the bedroom if you read before sleep. Create a reading chair — a specific spot associated only with books. The goal is to make books the most available thing in your physical field, not the least.

3. The 20-page habit

The most common reading goal is too ambitious. "Read for an hour a day" sounds reasonable but requires clearing an hour — which most days doesn't happen. "Read twenty pages" is different. Twenty pages takes fifteen to twenty minutes. It fits into the gaps of nearly any day.

The twenty-page habit has another virtue: it's hard to stop at twenty pages. Once you're in a book, you often keep reading. The minimum creates the start, and momentum does the rest.

4. Track what you read

Goodreads is the most popular tool for this, but a simple notebook works just as well. The act of recording a finished book — title, author, one sentence on what you took from it — produces a satisfaction that reinforces the habit. It also creates a reference: you can look back at what you read two years ago and remember what shaped your thinking.

Tracking has a subtle motivational effect: once you have a streak of finished books, you feel the social proof of your own past behaviour. "I read twenty-two books last year" is evidence that you are, in fact, a reader.

Choose Your Reading Wisely

5. The 50-page rule for quitting bad books

The most underrated productivity tip in reading is permission to quit. Most readers finish bad books out of a misapplied sense of obligation. This is a mistake. Every hour you spend grinding through a book you're not enjoying is an hour you didn't spend on a book that would have lit you up.

The 50-page rule: give any book fifty pages. If it hasn't engaged you by then, put it down without guilt and move to something else. Some readers use the formula "100 minus your age" — the older you get, the fewer pages you owe a book before moving on.

Your reading list is an opportunity cost. Every book you're reading is a book you're not reading. Choose accordingly.

6. Read summaries to filter

A well-written book summary — 2,000 to 4,000 words capturing the key ideas and arguments — can tell you in twenty minutes whether a book deserves your full attention. For a book that doesn't make the cut, the summary may be sufficient. For one that does, the summary serves as a primer that makes the full book faster to read.

This is what Actionable Books exists for. The summaries aren't a substitute for reading — they're a filter that helps you spend your reading hours where they'll return the most.

7. Annual re-reads of key books

The books worth re-reading are the ones that changed how you think. You were a different person when you read them the first time. Re-reading them with new experience and questions produces a different book — because you're different.

Most serious readers have a short list of books they return to every few years: their personal canon. Building this list is a practice. After finishing any book, ask: "Would I benefit from reading this again in five years?" If yes, note it. The re-read often yields more than the first read.

Solve the Time Problem

8. Audiobooks count

There is a persistent and largely groundless snobbery about audiobooks: the idea that listening doesn't "really" count as reading. Research doesn't support this. Comprehension rates for audiobooks and print books are comparable for most content, and the emotional and intellectual engagement is real.

Audiobooks work for time that reading doesn't reach: commutes, workouts, cooking, cleaning. At 1.25x or 1.5x speed (the sweet spot for most listeners before comprehension drops) a six-hour audiobook fits into a couple of commuting weeks. For readers who struggle to find sitting-still time, audiobooks are not a compromise — they're an unlock.

Audible, Libro.fm (which supports independent bookshops), and Spotify all have strong libraries. Libraries increasingly offer audiobooks free through the Libby app.

9. Reading before bed vs. reading in the morning

Both work; the question is which works for you.

Morning reading, before email and decisions have taken a toll on your cognitive resources, produces better retention and deeper engagement. The first thirty minutes of the day — reading with coffee, before looking at a phone — is consistently cited by serious readers as their most productive reading time.

Bedtime reading works as a wind-down ritual but has a limitation: many readers fall asleep within twenty minutes. If you're using your bedtime reading slot to make progress through difficult books, you'll find it slow going. Save difficult books for mornings. Save lighter reading for nights.

10. Library cards and Kindle Unlimited

Cost is a real barrier to reading more for many people, and it's the most unnecessary one. A library card gives you free access to an enormous collection of physical books, ebooks through the Libby app, and increasingly audiobooks. Most libraries also offer interlibrary loan, which means if your local branch doesn't have something, they can get it.

Kindle Unlimited (roughly £10/month) makes sense for readers who consume large quantities of books and read primarily on digital devices. It's less useful for selective readers who read fewer than five or six books per month, since the per-book cost doesn't beat buying used copies.

11. Digital versus physical books

The honest answer: it depends entirely on how you read.

Physical books produce better retention for many readers — possibly because of the physical context cues (where you were in the book, what the page looked like) that aid memory. They're better for marking up, for returning to passages, and for the physical pleasure of reading.

Digital books are better for reading in the dark, searching for passages, and having your entire library in one device. They're particularly good for non-fiction where you're looking for specific ideas rather than narrative flow.

Many serious readers use both: physical for books they know they'll want to mark, return to, and keep; digital for books they're less sure about, and for travel.

Make It Social and Accountable

12. Group reading and accountability

The easiest way to read more is to make a public commitment to it. A book club, even an informal one with two or three friends, adds social accountability to the reading habit. The conversation you'll have about the book motivates you to finish it. The social ritual of discussing ideas reinforces the habit.

Book clubs work best with a few constraints: a manageable cadence (monthly, not weekly), books short enough that busy people can realistically finish them, and meetings with a specific question or theme to discuss rather than open-ended "what did everyone think?"

If a formal book club is too much, a reading accountability partner — someone you check in with weekly about what you're reading — is simpler and often more effective.

13. Speed reading vs. deep reading — what actually works

Speed reading courses promise to triple or quadruple your reading pace through techniques like eliminating subvocalisation (the inner voice that "reads" the words). The research is sceptical. Most techniques that dramatically increase reading speed come at a cost to comprehension.

What does work: reading more actively (pausing to summarise, asking questions of the text, relating what you're reading to what you already know) increases both speed and retention over time. Readers who have read a lot in a domain read faster in that domain because they bring more context to each sentence.

The realistic goal is not to read twice as fast — it's to read more pages per week by building the habit, choosing the right books, and making space for it in your days.

14. How to read two books at once

Reading multiple books simultaneously is a productivity technique that divides opinion. For some readers, it causes confusion and abandonment. For others, it's the only way to sustain momentum — because when one book isn't calling you, the other is.

The rule that makes it work: keep books in different categories. A non-fiction book and a novel. A challenging book and an easy one. A professional development book and something purely for pleasure. The genres don't compete cognitively because they occupy different mental registers.

15. How to retain more of what you read

Reading more is only worthwhile if you retain something from what you read. The strategies that work:

Highlight actively, but lightly. Too much highlighting is as useless as none at all. Limit yourself to the sentences that genuinely surprise you or that you want to be able to find again. A highlighter that's too generous is the reading equivalent of copy-pasting without understanding.

Write a brief summary when you finish. It doesn't need to be long — three to five bullet points capturing the key ideas and one action you'll take. The act of summarising forces retrieval, which is the mechanism of memory consolidation.

Share what you learn. Explaining an idea to someone else is the most reliable way to find out whether you actually understand it. Talking about books — with a partner, in a group, on social media — strengthens retention and generates new understanding through conversation.

Revisit your highlights. Apps like Readwise automatically resurface highlights from books you've read on a spaced repetition schedule. For readers who use Kindle and take notes, it's one of the most effective retention tools available.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many books should I aim to read per year?

The honest answer is: more than you read last year. The specific number matters less than the trajectory. If you read two books last year, reading five this year is a meaningful improvement. The goal isn't to hit an arbitrary number — it's to build a practice that becomes self-sustaining.

That said: most serious, busy readers settle into a rhythm of one to two books per month (12-24 per year) as a sustainable target. More than that often means reading shallowly.

Is it better to read fast or slow?

Deep reading — slow, careful, engaging fully with the argument — produces better retention and deeper understanding. Speed reading produces the feeling of having covered ground without necessarily producing knowledge.

That said, not every book deserves the same depth. A book you're reading to extract one specific idea can be skimmed strategically. A book that's changing how you think deserves slow, careful attention.

What's the best time of day to read?

Morning, before email and other demands compete for attention, is consistently the most productive reading time for people who have the flexibility to use it. The cognitive resources needed for careful reading are highest early in the day.

Evening reading works well as a wind-down practice but is less suited to difficult material because mental fatigue affects comprehension and retention.

How do I read more when I have young children or a demanding job?

The two most reliable strategies: audiobooks for the time that's already accounted for (commutes, walks, cooking), and the twenty-page minimum before bed. Twenty pages takes fifteen minutes. You have fifteen minutes. The question is whether you use them before or after picking up your phone.

Why do I buy books but never read them?

This is known as the "tsundoku" phenomenon in Japanese — the accumulation of unread books. The causes vary: books bought in the enthusiasm of a recommendation without a specific reading plan, physical or digital libraries so large that choice paralysis sets in, or books acquired as a form of aspirational self-image rather than genuine reading intent.

The fix is to work from a single "next book" queue rather than a sprawling wishlist. When you finish a book, you choose the next one and only the next one. Decision fatigue disappears when the question isn't "what should I read?" but "am I ready to start the book that's next in line?"
